groGo


A mobile application that streamlines in-store grocery shopping experience.



Role

UX project owner

Duration

Sep 2018 - Jan 2019

Project Type

Course project

Tools

Axure RP 9 Beta, ShareX

THE PROBLEM

Shopping for groceries in the store takes too much time


Grocery shopping is a recurring task that almost no household could excape. However, not everyone enjoys doing it.


I almost always ended up spending much more time than I wanted shopping at the grocery store.


According to The Time Use Institute (2008) survey, US shoppers spend an hour per week shopping for groceries. However, in-store grocery shopping is still preferred over online shopping by 92% of shoppers in the US (ICSC,2018). Is there a way to make in-store grocery shopping enjoyable yet efficient?

For the individual project of the course Interaction Design, I aimed to design a solution that streamlines grocery shopping expeirence, giving shoppers back time lost during in-store grocery shopping.




THE SOLUTION

groGo - A planner, a trainer, and a navigator


The final solution is a mobile application -- groGo, which targets in-store grocery shoppers who wish their shopping trips could be more efficient. From grocery planning to expense tracking, the solution will serve as a planner, a trainer, and a navigator throughout the whole grocery journey.


Favorites


Pain point:

Making a well-planned shopping list takes too muchtime.


Solution:

Store and organize your favorite or frequently purchased items to efficiently plan for the next shopping trip.

In-Store Map


Pain point:

Running past the aisle where an item is located

Solution:

Generate in-store map and an optimized shopping route from your shopping list.

GO Mode


Pain point:

Bad spacial ability results in getting lost in the store.

Solution:

Navigate with AR view or traditional map view while you shop in-store.

Scan it!


Pain point:

Reading food labels and comparing similar items are tenious

Solution:

No more squinting! Let the app read food labels for you and help you meet your dietary goals.

Gro Point System


Pain point:

Lack of motivation to use an app

Solution:

Collect exclusive coupons by reaching your monthly shopping goals or by completing shopping challenges.



THE PROCESS

From exploring the possibilities to crafting the design


USER RESEARCH

What do people have to say about their shopping experience?


In order to understand people's pain points doing grocery shopping, I collected and analyzed both quantitative and qualitative data for further ideation.



Small Sample Survey


A user needs survey was conducted online with 16 volunteers to learn the in-store shoppers' challenges. 15 out of 16 respondants were the primary shopper of their households. The survey results showed that 10 out of 16 people reported to have often or always stayed longer than they expetected shopping for groceries. These people spend an average of 53 minutes per week for grocery shopping (Mdn: 52.5). In addition, these 10 people have reported to consider budget-freindliness, distance from the stores, and the variety of product selection when they do grocery shopping.





In addition, three issues have almost sometimes, very often, or always happened to the 10 respondents who reported to spend more time than they wanted during their shopping expereince:





Having to return to previous aisles for a forgotten item



Difficulty locating an item in the store



Comparing similar items for too long




User Interviews

In addition to the survey, I also interviewed four different shoppers individually. By having them walk me through their last grocery shopping experience, I uncovered different shoppers' motivations, grocery shopping styles, as well as other pain points related to inefficient shopping.


My husband is vegetarian, so I have to pay extra attention to what I buy.

I have trouble finding a particular item.

Food labels are too small. I spend too much time reading them.

I am buying much more than needed...I wish I could just not spend too much money.



Causal Diagram


From both the survey and interview, I was able to outline the reasons contributing to inefficient shopping experience.




COMPATITIVE ANALYSIS

What are the solutions out there that tackle smilar problems?




Aside from preliminary user research, I also examined existing products on the market, including grocery stores' mobile applications, as well as to-do list and meal prep apps. From the analysis, I noticed that grocery store apps have less sophisticated planning features, while meal prep apps or to-do list apps rarely include specific product information. The results implied a possible solution that includes the good of both application types.



DEFINE AND SCOPE

Setting up design objective and goals

To synthesize the research and interview findings, I have collected all root causes regarding grocery shopping from user research. Since traffic issues such as waiting for the bus or waiting in traffic may drastically affect the scope of project and would be better solved with existing applications, I decided to tackle issues that are related to execution time and decision time.





I also determined the directions of the design to help users reach their goals while filling the white spaces on the market.





Time & Money Saving


Help shoppers quickly make efficient and budget-minded purchase decisions.


Non Store-Specific


Incorporate data from different brands instead of a single store.


Dietary Preference


Identify suitable choices for shoppers to reach dietary goals.

IDEATE

How can I empower in-store shoppers with the capability to make informed purchase decisions without spending much time?


Brainstorming


I started to brainstorm as many potential solutions as possible. These include pickup service, hired shopper service, smart shopping cart, digital shelves, and interactive information wall at store.

In the end, I have considered user needs and contraints, and opted for designing 'groGo'-- a mobile app that serves as a one-stop soltution for in-store shoppers. The considerations are accessibiliy, maintainence, and development time.



I brainstormed eight possible features for the mobile app, and visualized them in storyboards depicting their usage scenarios.






After evaluation, I have rescoped and refined the functionality of the mobile solution in order to ensure most pain points in-store grovcery shoppers might face can be addressed.







Storymaps



To visualize important tasks, I developed a series of storymaps for the primary and secondary personas, each includes three layers: activities, tasks, and subtasks.

Usage Flow


With the story maps as guidance, I systemized the features in the form of an information architecture flow chart.

From the flow chart, I have identified four most frequently used features to be displayed at the most salient places on the app interface: search, shopping list, scanner, and GO mode.



PROTOTYPE & TEST

Translating ideas to assessible design



Paper Prototype


Before delving into digital prototype, a paper-based prototype model was first created to allow quick user testing and conceptualization.






QOC Analysis


To make design decisions, I adopted the QOC analysis method to reach the most optimal design option by taking important critera into consideration when evaluating options that address a design question.





User Testing


Time to put my first design to test and collect feedback! The paper prototype has undergone 6 user testing sessions.


Based on user feedback, I have made several improvements:

  • Changed the name of some features to avoid confision, e.g., 'item group' --> 'favorites'.
  • Changed design for scalability consideration, e.g., use carousel presentation for frequently visited stores.
  • Changed visual affordances such as icon size to improve usability.




Digital Prototype


For the digital prototype, I developed a color scheme and style guide for the application's visual elements.



The color blue is selected as the parimary hue of the palette, as blue light can activate parts of the brain related to attention and alterness (Lockley et al., 2006; Lehrl et al., 2007), which may contribute to better efficiency when users perform tasks such as searching for grocery items, planning shopping list and navigation in the store.




Design Changes


I have iterated multiple times working on the digital prototype, each time followed by a brief user testing on certain task for further feedback and usability check. Some key changes I have made before reaching the final design include:

Re-designed the dashboard on the home page


Before:

Plain numeric display for progress, budget and shopping time.

After:

Add progress bar for GO points to motivate users. Display user's own goal at the bottom of current expenses and shopping time, with trend tracking feature to check historic performance.

Changed the concept of features to match with real world experience


Before:

Same button design for 'add to list' and 'add to group'. Users had problem differentiating both concepts, and they were prone to tap the wrong button.

After:

Change the term 'group' to 'favorites' to avoid misunderstanding. Use a heart-shaped icon to signify it's a long-term, regularly purchsed item.

Redesigned the shopping list on in-store map


Before:

Items were presented in a horizontal scroll menu, ordered by aisle number.

After:

Use a minimizable, vertical menu to match the shopping list page design. Allow users to check individual item on the route by tapping the pin icon.





REFLECTIONS

Minimizing myself as a user during the process


Since the project stemmed from my personal painful experience when doing grocery, I would have mistaken my own problem to be everyone else's problem. It is however through user survey and interviews that I gradually delineated possible different users and their journey, as well as their true needs. Similarly, I had too many ideas upfront and were trying to realize all of them in my design. However, through the process of definition, user flow generation, and multiple testings and iterations, I learned the more is not always the better from my users, especially when my solution is specifically designed to save people time.

Brianna Brown


Scenario


It’s Saturday morning. Brianna wakes up straight at 6 and still feels tired – she had been hanging out with her coworkers all night. She takes a look at her schedule and gets up immediately. ‘Time waits no man,’ she thinks, with strong determination to complete all her chores by lunch.

While having breakfast, she browses groGo – her grocery shopping helper, and swiftly adds different pre-established item groups to a newly generated list. The App suggests that traffic at her nearby store will be busy at 9:30. Without further due, she quickly changed and leaves her apartment.

Upon arrival, Brianna activates the GO mode and lets groGo take the lead. Before she starts moving, groGo pops up a challenge ‘Finish shopping in 40 minute, and win a coupon!’ What a piece of cake, Brianna thinks. Within 20 minutes, she already picks up enough food for 12 meals thanks to groGo’s AR map feature. With the help of the ingredient scanner, she also dodges a few high-carb items that are on the list, and opts for lower ones.

Coming home, Brianna feels happy that she has a good start of the day. She completes her shopping 20 minutes sooner than before, and earns an exciting coupon just because of that. ‘Time is money’, she firmly believes it.

Kay Lam


Scenario


It’s Friday afternoon. Kay has no class and is excited about her yet another weekly grocery shopping trip. A busy student, Kay loves to enjoy her grocery shopping experience. For her, being able to learn all different kinds of food a US supermarket can offer is an amazing feeling – she can browse around and shop on and on, as if the shelves are high-end boutiques at Avenue des Champs-Élysées.

As usual, she does not have in mind what to buy, but a rough idea of what types of food she would need for the next week. ‘Surprise me, my store!’ she thinks.

After picking up some veggies and meat, she arrives at the international shelf. And suddenly, she spots a brand new product – Thai red curry paste. She loves Thai food and has always thought about making it by herself. Allergic to nuts, however, she isn’t sure whether the paste will be safe.

She opens her groGo app and activates her ingredient scannr. Within a second, the screen shows that the food is safe for her, based on a customized allergic list she has input before. On the app, she also learns that the product has over 4.5/5 rating by other customers. ‘This must be good!’ she thinks, and puts the item in her cart exciting to see how tasty curry she can make.

Scenario

Usually, she purchases nonperishable food like tomato sauce online – it’s cheaper, and saves time and effort. Standing in front of the tomato sauce shelf, she starts to feel confused. ‘This is why I prefer shopping online...the products all look too alike!’ Determined to buy the tomato sauce she used to get, she opens groGo, and types in the name of the product. After activating navigation feature, the product is highlighted on the screen. Lilian goes home relaxed and relieved.

Samuel Jones


Scenario


As a busy breadwinner, Sam is excited about the dinner tonight. It’s his birthday, and instead of going out to a fancy restaurant, his wife Grace suggested to make a candle light dinner for him. ‘My girl is always so romantic!’ he smiles as he thinks about it.

Maria Watson


Scenario


After another late night study, Maria wakes up on Saturday at noon. She has nothing to eat, and just as she’s about to open her Meijer app for yet another online ordering, she recalls her friends recommending her of the groGo app while persuading her to go shopping with them. ‘Okay..let me take a look and see what this app is all about. Maybe it’s not a bad idea to go shopping.’
