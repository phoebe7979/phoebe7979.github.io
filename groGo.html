The Project

Streamlining grocery shopping experience


Grocery shopping is a recurring task that almost no household could excape. However, not everyone enjoys doing it.


"I almost always ended up spending much more time than I wanted shopping at the grocery store."

According to The Time Use Institute (2008) survey, US shoppers spend an hour per week shopping for groceries. As time-consuming as it seems, in-store grocery shopping is still preferred over online shopping by 92% of shoppers in the US (ICSC,2018). Undoubtedly, shopping in physical stores allows one to have more control of what he/she gets -- you can handpick the freshest looking produce, or spot special deals that are not listed online. But what about control over time and budget? In fact, based on the results of a small-scaled questionnaire with 16 participants, 10 out of 16 people reported to have often or always stayed longer than they expected shopping for groceries. Is there a way to make in-store grocery shopping enjoyable yet efficient?

For the individual project of the course Interaction Design, I aimed to design a solution that streamlines grocery shopping expeirence, helping in-store shoppers manage time and money more effectively during their shopping routines.


The Final Design

A planner, a trainer, and a navigator


The final solution is a mobile application -- groGo, which targets in-store grocery shoppers who wish their shopping trips could be more efficient. From grocery planning to expense tracking, the solution will serve as a planner, a trainer, and a navigator throughout the whole grocery journey. Unlike most other products on the market, it does not restrict itself to any specific grocery store, but instead leverages its powerful database that integrates product information across different grocery brands.



Throught the research and comparative evaluation process, I have identified several pain points for frequent in-store shoppers.

Maintaining shopping list is a hassle...why do I have to build a whole list again everytime?

I have to travel between different aisles to look for a missing item on my list.

Even some stores provide digital maps, I have trouble oreinting myself.

Food labels are too small. I spend too much time reading them.

I rarely complete my goals.


groGo is designed to address them with 5 key features.


Favorites

Store and organize your favorite or frequently purchased items to efficiently plan for the next shopping trip.

In-Store Map

Generate in-store map and an optimized shopping route from your shopping list.

Go Mode

Navigate with AR view or traditional map view while you shop in-store.

Scan it!

No more squinting! Let the app read food labels for you and help you meet your dietary goals.

Gro Point System

Collect exclusive coupons by reaching your monthly shopping goals or by completing shopping challenges.

In order to understand people's pain points doing grocery shopping, I collected and analyzed both quantitative and qualitative data for further ideation.



1. Small Sample Survey


A short user needs survey was conducted online to learn the in-store shoppers' challenges. The reponses from five-point Likert scaled questions showed that 10 out of 16 people reported to have often or always stayed longer than they expetected shopping for groceries . Additionally, three problems have often or always happened to more than half of the 10 respondents during their shopping expereince:


  • Having to return to previous aisles because of missing an item
  • Having difficulty locating an item in the store
  • Spending a lot of time comparing similar products


2. User Interviews


In addition to the survey, I also interviewed four different shoppers individually. By having them walk me through their last grocery shopping experience, I have come to understand that not everyone is looking for efficient shopping: for example, one of the interviewee described how she actually indulges herself spending quite some time browsing grocery items in store, because that is one of the few enjoyments she could have living a busy life. Some of the new findings that emerged from the interviews are:


  • Shoppers who only shop for themselves are buying much more than needed
  • Some shoppers would choose to shop online once a while because there is a promotion
  • One shopper has a different diet from her husband, and would thus have to pay extra attention to food labels during shopping


3. Competitive Analysis


Aside from preliminary user research, I also examined existing products on the markets, including grocery stores' mobile applications, as well as to-do list and meal prep apps. From the analysis, I noticed that grocery store apps have less sophisticated planning features, while meal prep apps or to-do list apps rarely include specific product information. The results implied a possible solution that includes the good of both application types.



4. Personas


From the user research, I developed a set of different personas for reference during the design. I then envisioned how the primary, secondary, and supplemental personas would use the final product.


BRIANNA BROWN - SCENARIO


It’s Saturday morning. Brianna wakes up straight at 6 and still feels tired – she had been hanging out with her coworkers all night. She takes a look at her schedule and gets up immediately. ‘Time waits no man,’ she thinks, with strong determination to complete all her chores by lunch.

While having breakfast, she browses groGo – her grocery shopping helper, and swiftly adds different pre-established item groups to a newly generated list. The App suggests that traffic at her nearby store will be busy at 9:30. Without further due, she quickly changed and leaves her apartment.

BRIANNA BROWN - SCENARIO (cont.)


Upon arrival, Brianna activates the GO mode and lets groGo take the lead. Before she starts moving, groGo pops up a challenge ‘Finish shopping in 40 minute, and win a coupon!’ What a piece of cake, Brianna thinks. Within 20 minutes, she already picks up enough food for 12 meals thanks to groGo’s AR map feature. With the help of the ingredient scanner, she also dodges a few high-carb items that are on the list, and opts for lower ones.

Coming home, Brianne feels happy that she has a good start of the day. She completes her shopping 20 minutes sooner than before, and earns an exciting coupon just because of that. ‘Time is money’, she firmly believes it.

KAY LAM- SCENARIO


It’s Friday afternoon. Kay has no class and is excited about her yet another weekly grocery shopping trip. A busy student, Kay loves to enjoy her grocery shopping experience. For her, being able to learn all different kinds of food a US supermarket can offer is an amazing feeling – she can browse around and shop on and on, as if the shelves are high-end boutiques at Avenue des Champs-Élysées.

As usual, she does not have in mind what to buy, but a rough idea of what types of food she would need for the next week. ‘Surprise me, my store!’ she thinks.

KAY LAM- SCENARIO (cont.)


After picking up some veggies and meat, she arrives at the international shelf. And suddenly, she spots a brand new product – Thai red curry paste. She loves Thai food and has always thought about making it by herself. Allergic to nuts, however, she isn’t sure whether the paste will be safe.

She opens her groGo app and activates her ingredient scannr. Within a second, the screen shows that the food is safe for her, based on a customized allergic list she has input before. On the app, she also learns that the product has over 4.5/5 rating by other customers. ‘This must be good!’ she thinks, and puts the item in her cart exciting to see how tasty curry she can make.

LILIAN SMITH - SCENARIO


It’s Wednesday afternoon. Lilian is preparing for a big dinner tonight because her husband is inviting some friends over. While cooking, she realizes that the tomato sauce is out. Usually, she purchases nonperishable food like tomato sauce online – it’s cheaper, and saves time and effort. Now that since she doesn’t want to let people down by changing the entre menu, she decides to bike to a supermarket.

Standing in front of the tomato sauce shelf, she starts to feel confused. ‘This is why I prefer shopping online...the products all look too alike!’ Determined to buy the tomato sauce she used to get, she opens groGo, and types in the name of the product. After activating navigation feature, the product is highlighted on the screen. Lilian goes home relaxed and relieved.

From ideas to final prototype, the design process includes several steps:



1. Brainstorming


Before deciding on designing a mobile app, I started to brainstorm as many potential solutions as possible, no matter whether the solution already exists on market or not. For example, one potential solution is pickup service, where users can select desired items prior to their shopping trips and pick up groceries once they arrive. This solution balances users' need for saving time and having fresher food, but the incurred service fee may not save shoppers money. Some other brainstormed solutions include hired shopper service, smart shopping cart, digital shelves, and interactive infomation wall at store.

In the end, I have considered user needs and contraints, and opted for designing 'groGo'-- a mobile app that serves as a one-stop soltution for in-store shoppers. The considerations are as follows:




Accessibility


Other solutions requires services fee or depend on the availability of the hardware devices. In comparison, mobile applications provides easier access to the users.


Development Time

Some solutions require participation from different grocery brands. It may take way more time for such services to be launched in most stores in the country.


Maintenance

For solutions such as smart shopping cart, digital shelves, and interactive information walls, the cost and time for maintenance is significantly more than a mobile app.


I then utilized storyboards to visualize possible usage scenarios for each possible feature on the app.




In order to conduct more detailed tasks analysis for each persona concerning how they would use groGo, I developed a series of storymaps for each persona, each includes three layers: activities, tasks, and subtasks.

From these maps, I am able to distill a common path, which allows me to further develope a more granular architecture of the mobile appplication.


2. Usage Flow


With the story maps as guidance, I was then able to systemize the features in the form of an information architecture flow chart. With such visualization as guidance, the scope of the product were made much clearer.

In addition, the flow chart allows me to identify and include different use cases for the application, while determining which features should be made the most accessible on the interface. From the flow chart, I have identified four most frequently used features to be displayed at the most salient places on the app interface: search, shopping list, scanner, and GO mode. Thse features were mentioned as the first few steps in the persona's scenarios. For example, in one of the story map, Kay, the secondary persona, spots a new item that she is intersted in. In this case, she would want to quickly check the ingredients to see if the product contains nuts. Instead of having to enter the GO mode, a scanner should be made instantly availble when she opens the app. This is why the scanner app is designed to be one of the tabs in the bottom menu.



Of course, the flow chart is not perfect, and the actual user flow of the app has been refined over the course of development. One process that I did not think very thoroughly during this step was how users import their favorite items to the shopping list. Additionally, I have also simplified the scanner experience to reduce interaction cost in the prototype phase. page

After it's done, the paper prototype has undergone 6 user testings. Based on user feedback, I have made several improvements:

  • Changed the name of some features to avoid confision, e.g., 'item group' --> 'favorites'.
  • Changed design for scalability consideration, e.g., use carousel presentation for frequently visited stores.
  • Changed visual affordances such as icon size to improve usability.


4. Digital Iterations


From wireframe to the final design, I have iterate multiple times for the digital prototype, each time followed by a brief user testing for further feedback.

The prototype has had 6 different version updates by the end of the project.
