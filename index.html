Contextual Effects on Mandarin Biased Heterographic Homophone Resolution

Abstract

The present study aims at exploring the effects of contextual constraints on the resolution of Mandarin biased heterographic homophones. Through two cross-modal naming tasks, we purported to answer the following research questions: (1) Does relative frequency affect meaning activation in a neutral context? (2) Do local contextual constraints influence meaning activation at the early stage of biased heterographic homophone resolution? (3) Does the inclusion of a global context have an early effect during ambiguity resolution?

The results indicated that relative meaning frequency does not affect meaning activation. In the neutral context, naming times for both the dominant and the subordinate meanings did not differ significantly. Secondly, strong local contextual constraints could lead to selective activation of the correct meaning, while inhibiting the activation of improper meanings. Without contextual support, even the dominant meaning of a biased heterographic homophone could not be successfully activated. Thirdly, providing discourse constraints did not facilitate activation of the correct meaning, but rather led to an overall latency in naming times.

The results of the study disconfirm the Modular Hypothesis, which opposes an early effect of contextual constraints. Additionally, the results cannot be explained by the Reordered Access Model, which argues against selective access of the correct meaning by contextual information. Our results are most compatible with the Context Sensitive Model (e.g., Vu et al., 2000). According to this model, a strong context can activate the single meaning it supports. Nevertheless, our results suggest that global context does not yield additional effects when local constraints are sufficient. This may be caused by the fact that global context does not have an early effect at all, or that global constraints do not exert extra effects when there are enough constraints in the local context.

Graduate Thesis

Yi-Chun Chen (2017). Contextual Effects on Mandarin Biased Heterographic Homophone Resolution (Master’s thesis). National Taiwan Normal University, Taipei, Taiwan.

The Representation of Polysemy in Mandarin Verbs: Chī, Dǎ, and Xǐ

Abstract

This paper reports the findings from a semantic judgment priming task aimed at examining how senses of three semantically complex Mandarin verbs chī ‘eat’, dǎ ‘hit’, and xǐ ‘wash’ are stored in the mental lexicon. Although the prime stimuli belonged to the basic senses of the three critical polysemous verbs, three conditions of targets, that is, verb phrases with the basic sense (e.g., chī niúpái ‘to eat steak’), closely related senses (e.g., chī wěiyá ‘to attend a year-end party’), and distantly related senses (e.g., chī lǎoběn ‘to live on one’s own fat’), were prepared to test whether processing patterns varied for different types of extended senses within a polysemous verb. The results indicate linearly decreasing priming effects for senses moving from the core senses, through the closely related senses, to the distantly related senses. These effects conflict with the separate-entry view and provide evidence for a shared core representation among polysemous senses.

Journal Article

Hsiao, Huichen S., Yi-Chun, Chen and Ying-Chen Wu. (2016) The Representation of Polysemy in Mandarin Verbs: Chī, Dǎ, and Xǐ. Concentric: Studies in Linguistics, 42(1), 1-30.

Effects of Metaphorical Awareness on Reading Comprehension and Vocabulary Retention

Abstract

The present study takes vocabulary from the metaphor “BUSINESS IS WAR” as an example to explore the effects of metaphor as supplemented learning strategy on learning. Previous studies have indicated the facilitation effects of metaphor awareness on short-term and long-term vocabulary and idiom acquisition (Boers, 2000b; Guo, 2007). Still, some researches have shown that learners are not able to utilize the enhanced awareness of conceptual metaphor for the comprehension of newly encountered words and expressions (Boers, 2000a; Condon, 2008), or to categorize new vocabulary into existed principles (Beréndi et al., 2008). Our research reports a learning experiment that examines whether enhancing metaphoric awareness facilitates reading comprehension of business-related passages and learning outcomes of Mandarin business vocabulary.


The results showed that (i) overally, the experimental group outperformed the control group in the posttest; (ii) after the learning phase, both the experimental group and the control group have demonstrated significant growth in terms of the vocabulary knowledge of the target words, but no significant difference was found between the two groups; (iii) in the blank-filling task in the delayed-posttest, the experimental group had significantly more corrected answers than the controls, and this advantage was also found for the new metaphoric nouns. The results indicated that awareness of cognitive metaphor cannot immediately enhance reading comprehension of the learners, but can be facilitative for the learning and memory of metaphoric vocabulary.

Conference Paper

Hsiao, S. H., & Chen, Y. Effects of Metaphorical Awareness on Reading Comprehension and Vocabulary Retention. Presented at the 2015 International Annual Conference of Teaching Chinese as a Second Language in Taitung, Taiwan. (Dec 25 to Dec 27, 2015)

Event Design

Responsibility

As the marketing speciliast loking after the ANZ market, I was the main organizer of Synology 2018 Syndey, the company's Sydney annual user event. My job was to design the whole event, including the show schedule, logistics, event venue planning. I do work with a local event agency which was in charge of coordination and communication with the venue, the AV team, as well as printing vendors.

Challenges

For this event, I would need to plan and design a space that is able to accomodate up to 400 people, as well as retaining them throughout the whole event. Additionally, the design should be able to demonstrate sponsor booths from four different external brans, hands-on experience booth of five various solution themes, new hardware products for the company, three food stations, as well as an activity area.

Solution

In order to keep every attendee interested until the last minute of the show, I have opted for an extremely spacious hotel venue capable of accommodating all activity spaces.

The whole event space was divided into 6 areas:

Registration: The registration was located ourside of the event center, facing the main entrance of the hotel building. With pull-up banners explicitly indicating its function and badges displayed on the table, attendees would proactively come over to register. To provide smooth experience for the attendees, an electronic check-in mobile app was adopted to quickly scan the QR-Code on each ticket.

Audio & Visual Control Area: Since the presentaions required a complicated streaming system to switch between different display modes, the A/V area was set up at the side of the stage so the coworker in charge could see both the presenter's laptop and the screens to ensure correct display.

Activity Area & New Product Area: Serving as room divider, a double-sided media wall was displayed at the back of the auditorium. One side was purported to engage attendees in some fun activities during the break, while the other side served as statis display for new products.

Sponsor Area: This was an area of four booths for sponsors to display their products. To provide an aligned visual image, the design team has made a template for all sponsor booth backdrops.

Demo Area: This was an area of five booths to display the company's new solutions. Each booth features a theme, with dedicated coworkers standing by to provide guidance on hands-on demonstration.

Food Area: The food area consisted of three food stations. It was located at the very back of the space after discussion with the hotel and event agency. This arrangement offered sufficient space for guests to line up for food, while providing equal visibility of both sponsor and demo areas.
