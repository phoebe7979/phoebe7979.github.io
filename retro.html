THE PROJECT

Enhancing meeting efficiency for 170 development teams at Ford by closely examining the productivity tool


For a course project, I worked in a team of four UX-focused students to provide usability consulting service for RetroQuest -- an open source web application developed by FordLabs for running weekly retrospecitves.

The web application’s current target users are internal teams at Ford. On a weekly basis, 90-170 teams at Ford run their retrospecitve meetings on RetroQuest. With only one designer in charge of the UX of the application, the development team is seeking consulting from our team to help them thoroughly evaluate the application's usability to boost retro efficiency for internal employees.

THE METHODOGY

Examining the web application via seven different approaches


We assessed RetroQuest comprehensively using different methods according to different research questions, gathering both quantitative and qualitative data to help us generate key recommendations for our clients.





INTERACTION MAP

What are all possible interactions on RetroQuest?


To better understand the application, we started by visualizing all possible interactions on the interface to learn how users can engage with the system in different ways.





While the application worked smoothly for the most part, we did find two apparent issues.

Clicking 'cancel' in the edit mode does not cancel the edit work.
Clicking 'Download CSV' button opens a blank page, only right-click works.


COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

How do the competitors approach similar features?



Next, we conducted a thorough analysis with 6 direct (FunRetro, GoReflect, ScatterSpoke) and partial (Trello, Basecamp, Airtable) competitors to see what they did well that can be receommended to RetroQuest.

We identified 6 great features that the competitors have and recommended them to RetroQuest:



Instead of logging to board, use an account system for all members to centralize board access.
Scatterspoke uses vote count limit per board member to help better identify most urgent issues.
Allow for some grouping for thematically similar cards by moving cards, adding tags, merging cards, or append to another card.
Showing card creator's identity avoids the 'silent moment' when the creator forgets about adding a card in discussion.
Use visualized analytics to reflect team sentiments, top contributors, progress status, and other trends.
Move the 'Download CSV' feature to a less prominent location to minimize elements on the board.

USER INTERVIEWS

Who are the users? How do they use RetroQuest? What are their motivations and frustrations?



In order to understand how users feel using RetroQuest, we conducted 5 semi-structured interviews with current users of different roles at Ford. The interview protocol can be found here .

From these interviews, we learned how users normally use RetroQuest in a retro. We also learned about the edge cases of the application.

SURVEY

What are users’ attitude about RetroQuest and proposed features?



With findings from the previous research, we have gathered several theories and assumptions that could help improve the application. To see whether these considerations are really needed by the majority of the users and their attitudes about them, we distributed a survey to employees at Ford.

Within 5 days, we received 81 responses. Using Tableau and excel, we visualized the data to easily interpret the results.





From the results, we have a better learning about the users:

  • Respondents that currently use RetroQuest are active on 1.41 team boards on average
  • Most respondents do not like to show their names on their cards, claiming that they do not have difficulty remembering creating a card.
  • Users prefer RetroQuest to be simple, without having too many features.


We have also identified 4 most requested features by RetroQuest users:

  • Be able to set a timer for each card to be aware of time
  • Sort all columns by clicking one button
  • Drag and drop cards across different columns and positions
  • Track team performance and sentiment with visual analytics

HEURISTICS EVALUATION

How many, if any, usability problems does RetroQuest have?



We constructed a set of 13 heuristics from Nielsen(1995), Farooq et al. (2008), and Hermann (2009) to examine RetroQuest from general user interface design point of view, and it's effectiveness in computer-supported cooperative work. The heuristics are:



  • Visibility of system status
  • Match between system and the real world
  • Communication around shared work should be able to happen in real time as the work is done
  • User control and freedom
  • Consistency and standards
  • Error prevention
  • Recognition rather than recall
  • Support flexibility in the granularity of planning
  • Aesthetic and minimalist design
  • Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors
  • Help and documentation
  • Support the big picture and visualization of rich material
  • Support merges and document them


In the end, we identified 18 violations, with 5 of them rated as most severe.

Aside from the recurring issues already idnetified in the previous research, we also found that a vote cannot be retracted after the user casts it. This prevents users from recovering from an accidental vote, futher resulting in inaccurate reflection of prioritzation.


Lack of unvote feature prevents error recovery.


PREFERENCE TEST

Evaluating the usability of two proposed desgin changes



So far, we have identified a number of UX issues and come up with design recommendations for them. Without putting these ideas into test, however, we wouldn't know if the proposed changes address users' desires and improve usability. Therefore, we took the chance of a four-day period to conduct a small preference test for two proposed changes:



(Left) The old design: users cannot take off a vote. (Right) The proposed design: added undo feature for the upvote button.


(Left) The old design: no salient way to infer meeting progress. (Right) The proposed design: added undo feature for the upvote button.


By using a set of modified SUS (System Usability Scale) questions, we gauged users' preference between the two design options.



Different from other studies we have done, the results were rather confusing -- we found no strong preference for one version over another, for both questions! At first, we were stunned and clueless, because our versions were designed exactly based on collected user needs. After referring to the free text entries and reexamining our methodology, we found that users were mostly critiquing the aesthetics of the designs, instead of the features usability of them.

Although we did not succssfully validate our assumptions, we have learned a lot from this experience. If we had the chance to do the test again, we would:

  • Use lower-fi mockups so participants focus less on the visual elements
  • Distribute the survey to more participants for more precise data
  • Conduct face-to-face interviews as well to ask more follow-up questions for deeper understanding




USABILITY TESTING

What parts of RetroQuest mobile view and new archive design work well? What do not work so well?



At this point, we have only examined issues on the desktop browser view. To understand how RetroQuest performs on the mobile view, as well as users' experience on the new interface in the archive page, we conducted a series of usability test with 5 users.



Each session lasted 30-40 minutes. On their own mobile phones, the participants performed 7 common tasks following a standard retrospective procedure. On the desktop browser, the participants performed one task with two subtasks to check historic board details. The whole testing script and task details can be accessed here.



We found two usability issues specifically related to the mobile view:



First, lack of feedback for card creation and retro ending process confused mobile users. During the test, all users had to double-check to make sure certain action is performed. We recommend adding a pop-up notification to update system status.



(Left) Users had to scroll down to make sure a card is created. (Right) Users had to switch to different tabs from action items to make sure all thoughts are cleared after hitting 'End Retro'.


Second, mobile configuration must be revisited. Four users have been observed to dwell longer on the tasks due to mobile keyboard or browser configurations.



(Left) After typing a thought, users tend to click the ‘down arrow’ in the extreme bottom left. They attempt to close the keyboard in doing so, thinking a submission button is hidden behind it. (Right) On iOS, ‘Return’ key on the bottom right submits a thought, but users looked for an ‘Enter’ key instead.
